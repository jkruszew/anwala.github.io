Winter Olympics 2018: Rexrode's diary goes into deep freeze

Joe Rexrode, USA TODAY NETWORK -- Tennessee Published 7:43 a.m. CT Feb. 12, 2018 | Updated 9:49 a.m. CT Feb. 12, 2018
CLOSE

#TeamTennessee has high aspirations for what sport they would compete in to bring home the gold. USA TODAY NETWORK - Tennessee staffers Joe Rexrode, Geoff Calkins and Andrew Nelles will be covering the 2018 Winter Olympics from PyeongChang. Ayrika Whitney/USA TODAY NETWORK - Tennessee

Tennessean sports columnist Joe Rexrode poses next to Soohorang the Tiger -- one of the Winter Olympics mascots -- on Monday in Pyeongchang, South Korea. A night-time assignment covering biathlon on what might end up the coldest day of the Games required maximum winter protection.(Photo: Josh Peter / USA Today)

CONNECTTWEETLINKEDINCOMMENTEMAILMORE

My phone said it was 14 degrees outside and that was a damn lie. Had to be. I would have guessed minus-10, with a wind chill of minus-112.

Then again, I had been psyched out all day, sitting in our 17th-floor apartment in one of countless high rises built for media at the Games, writing stories and listening to the wind howl and rattle our windows.

Several miles away, snowboarders fell left and right amid the conditions, a dangerous and stupid scene. Monday night was going to be it – the peak of the nastiest weather day of the entire Olympics, and my assignment was covering two biathlon events in the mountains. It was time to empty the suitcase.

CLOSE

Bitter temperatures and biting winds haunted athletes and fans alike during the second day of competition at the Pyeongcheng Olympics. (Feb. 12) AP

Because, you see, Sunday night may have been the coldest my body has felt in my entire life. And that’s my own fault. I had hockey duty all day, writing features on Jim Slater and Ryan Gunderson off the U.S. men’s practice and then covering the U.S. women in their opening win against Finland. So I figured, take buses, work in arenas, no problem. I wore standard winter gear. The problem came when the bus heading home from Kwandong Hockey Centre was 15 minutes late. Just 15 minutes in that cold and wind, at night, and my double-gloved fingertips were frozen. My legs, even with long johns and flannel-lined jeans on, were practically burning from the cold. My face could barely move.

A wimpy Nashvillian in the winter? Maybe, but I’ve lived more than half my life in Michigan, and I’ve spent time amid bitter conditions in Minnesota and Wisconsin.

This stuff around here is serious. It has a special kind of bite to it, as mentioned in any book or movie about the Korean War that you’ll find. When I first got to South Korea, I thought the weather concerns may have been a bit overblown.

You know, like the Zika virus in Rio. But no, it’s a legit concern, more like the Russian doping in Sochi. The good news is, the next week will see a warm front with highs on some days expected to exceed freezing. But I had to earn it.
